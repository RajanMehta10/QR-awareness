Think Before You Scan — QR Safety Guidance

Short, clear steps to stay safe when scanning QR codes on company premises.

Quick summary: QR codes are convenient but can be abused. Scanning an unknown or tampered QR may lead to phishing, malware, unwanted network connections or financial fraud. If you're not 100% sure who placed it — don't scan.

Why QR codes can be risky

  • Phishing & credential theft: QR can redirect to fake login pages that capture usernames and passwords.
  • Malware / malicious downloads: QR may point to software (APK) or exploit pages that try to install malware on your phone or device.
  • Auto-actions: Some QR payloads can trigger immediate actions — connect to Wi‑Fi, send email/SMS, start a call, or add calendar events.
  • Fraud & payments: QR can redirect you to fake payment pages or initiate transactions.
  • Tracking & data leakage: Opening links can leak device identifiers, location, or internal network info.
  • Sticker overlay attacks: Attackers place malicious QR stickers over legitimate codes (e.g., posters, menus).

Real-world example (brief)

  • A poster in a public area was over-stickered with a malicious QR. Employees who scanned were sent to a fake SSO page and entered credentials, enabling account takeover.

How to spot a suspicious QR

  • Unexpected placement (a new sticker over an old poster, handwritten codes, or codes placed in unusual locations).
  • Shortened or odd-looking URLs in the preview (e.g., bit.ly/... with no context).
  • Poor-quality printing, misaligned stickers, or codes without brand context on official-looking signage.
  • Offers that seem too good to be true (free gifts, instant refunds, prize claims).

Before you scan — quick checklist

Do
  • Verify the source: did Facilities, HR, or IT approve this sign?
  • Preview the link — use your camera or scanner that shows the full URL before opening.
  • If unsure, ask a colleague or contact IT before scanning.
  • Keep your device OS and security apps updated.
Don't
  • Don't install apps or device profiles prompted by a QR unless from a trusted source.
  • Don't connect to unknown Wi‑Fi networks offered via QR codes.
  • Don't enter usernames, passwords or payment details on pages you didn't explicitly verify.
  • Don't assume a QR is safe because it was placed in a public area.

If you accidentally scanned a suspicious QR

  1. Stop interacting with the page/app immediately (do not enter credentials or grant permissions).
  2. Disconnect from the network (turn off Wi‑Fi / mobile data) to limit potential data loss or command-and-control access.
  3. Run a full device scan with your antivirus/EDR. If your company uses an EDR (e.g., Sophos), notify them to run a check.
  4. Change passwords for any accounts you accessed from a different, secure device.
  5. Report the incident to IT/Security immediately and include the time, location, and a screenshot of the page you saw. If the QR requested installation, preserve the device and let IT advise next steps.
